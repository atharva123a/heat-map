Parallel Set Plot

Parallel plot or parallel coordinates plot allows to compare the feature of several individual observations (series) on a set of numeric variables. Each vertical bar represents a variable and often has its own scale. (The units can even be different). Values are then plotted as series of lines connected across each axis.

The Ã¬ris dataset provides four features (each represented with a vertical line) for 8 flower samples (each represented with a color line). Samples are grouped in Two species. The chart beside highlights efficiently. Here the Blue color represent the species " Setosa " and the orangish versicolor.

Let's consider two features: Sepal Length and Sepal Width. We observe that the Sepal Length of the Versicolor species is longer than that of Setosa.

In the case of the first three features, Sepal Length, Sepal Width, and Petal Length, we can make the following observations: For the Setosa species, as the Sepal Width increases, the Petal Length tends to decrease. In other words, Setosa flowers with wider sepals tend to have shorter petals. Conversely, for the Versicolor species, when the Sepal Width is shorter, the Petal Length tends to be longer. This means that Versicolor flowers with narrower sepals tend to have longer petals.

Let's consider the other two features: Petal Length and Petal Width. We observe that in the case of the Versicolor species, both Petal Length and Petal Width are longer and wider compared to Setosa

What for

A parallel plot allows to study the features of samples for several quantitative variables. Its strength is that the variables can even be completely different: different ranges and even different units. In the graphic above flower features were grouped in species, and all variables were normalized and sharing the same unit (cm).

Common Mistake

Like for line plot, displaying too many samples result in a cluttered and unreadable spaghetti chart. Sort the variables on the X axis, it makes sense to avoid crosses in sample lines. Try different scalings to find the one fitting your data best.
